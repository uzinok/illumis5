New findings on severe PMS - PMDD. Find out now how to avoid the dangerous disease!

Last modified on 5 days ago

Advertisement Promotion

Sarah Lafarge

Editor, Netmums Reviews

All women are more or less familiar with PMS - premenstrual syndrome, but few know: if you don't deal with the symptoms in time, you may have complications. Today we'll talk about premenstrual dysphoric disorder according to the latest research and its consequences - how they can drastically change your personal life and work sphere. This article is courtesy of Dr Leona Williams, an obstetrician-gynaecologist with over 20 years of experience.

What is the difference?

During PMS, women usually experience lower abdominal pain, mood deterioration, which makes it more difficult to communicate with close people, especially a partner in a relationship. And increased appetite or, conversely, reduced leads to problems with the figure. Along with this, headaches can be observed, which prevent normal work. With PMDD, women feel almost the same, but in an intensified form. Such a diagnosis is made in the presence of 5 or more symptoms from the entire list below:

  • feelings of anxiety, sadness, stress;
  • uncontrollable anger and depression;
  • suicidal thoughts;
  • panic attacks, uncontrollable crying;
  • mood swings, anger;
  • loss of interest in daily activities;
  • feelings of chronic fatigue and a sense of loss of control;
  • sleep disturbances and loss of control over attention;
  • gastrointestinal disorders: abdominal bloating, overeating;
  • headaches and joint pain.

Why does PMDD occur?

Scientists do not know the exact cause. However, most experts link the disorder to hormonal changes. Women who are more sensitive to changes in serotonin levels may be more likely to experience PMDD. Genetic predisposition to cancer and mental illness are also influential. Other studies have shown that in some cases, PMDD may be linked to stressful and traumatic past events, such as emotional or physical abuse. In other words, it is impossible to know for sure if PMS will lead to PMDD, so it is important to take care of your own health in advance.

Conclusion: PMDD is very dangerous to women's health.

How is PMDD treated?

There are many approaches to treatment. As Dr Leona Williams, an obstetrician-gynaecologist with more than 20 years of experience, points out, medications and lifestyle changes can be used. Medications include psychotropic drugs, hormone therapy, and oral contraception. But all these medications have terrible effects on the body! They disrupt the body's natural rhythm, forcing it to readjust.

In some women, especially at the beginning of treatment or when the dose is changed, the medication can cause suicidal thoughts. If antidepressants are used, they can cause physical and mental dependence, especially if taken for a long time or used incorrectly. It is also unknown if the remedy is suitable for you. Allergic reactions such as rashes, itching, swelling or difficulty breathing are therefore possible.

It is important to remember that all drugs have their side effects and the decision to start treatment should be made taking into account the benefits they may bring and the potential risks.

Non-medicinal methods include exercise, dietary changes, and yoga. However, all this does not completely eliminate symptoms, but only serves as an additional method of prevention.

How PMDD and PMS are detrimental to the workplace

Firstly, physical symptoms play a big role. Some women experience soreness, fatigue, headaches and muscle aches during PMS. This can reduce concentration and productivity at work.

Secondly, these are emotional changes: irritability, anxiety, depression and mood changes. Because of them, relationships with colleagues deteriorate, you quarrel and further damage your own nervous system.

Thirdly, self-esteem deteriorates. PMDD can cause women to feel inferior, dissatisfied and have lower self-esteem. As a result, your motivation decreases.

Finally, you may miss work due to extreme pain.

According to the YouGov survey, just over a third (35%) believe that pain during PMS affects their ability to work, making it difficult to concentrate. Around one in five (19%) have had to take a short break because of pain, and one in three (35%) have had to either go home early (17%) or take a day off work (18%) because of pain.

Your relationships with family and friends will become worse because of PMDD

This question has been researched by psychologists in conjunction with medical practitioners. Expert gynaecologist Leona Williams speaks from the experience of her patients.

Because of your aggression and irritability, you will snap at family members and reproach them for trifles. If you are a mum, your activity in family a ffairs and duties will fall heavily, which may cause frustration and dissatisfaction of other family members.

There will also be communication problems, leading to a breakdown in communication or increasing conflicts. Your sexual activity will also decrease: women with PMDD experience pain or discomfort during sexual intercourse. As a result, sexual tension and dissatisfaction with each other arise in the relationship.

You won't be able to exercise properly

Due to abdominal swelling, headaches and constant fatigue, your athletic performance will become very weak, your figure will lose its former lustre. Imagine that you have been pumping your buttocks for months, creating a slim waist - and that's it! PMDD will not allow you to stay fit.

In addition, even when there is no pain, there is an increased susceptibility to injury. This is due to decreased attention, reduced coordination and impaired reflexes.

Do painkillers help?

Unfortunately, not always. Many women use painkillers, but this is wrong. Pain is a signal from the body that cannot be ignored. It makes us realise what needs to be done. Painkillers can only bring a short-term effect, and if you use them often, you disrupt the hormonal balance, risk obesity. Painkillers also do not save you from stress, which prevents you from working effectively, exercising and spending time with friends.

Antidepressants and painkillers can cause drowsiness, dizziness, changes in appetite and weight, decreased libido, dry mouth, and other discomfort.

Ways to reduce PMS symptoms

- It is most important to get enough sleep and rest.
- Proper nutrition: increase your intake of foods rich in vitamins and minerals, especially calcium, magnesium and vitamin D. Minimise your intake of caffeine, salt and sugar.
- Exercise and physical activity will help improve your mood and reduce PMS symptoms.
- Avoid stress and learn relaxation techniques such as meditation or deep breathing.

What are the dangers of delayed treatment and how to avoid it

The BMC Women's Health 2023 study, states the following:

Many female patients felt that they had to prove to doctors how serious their symptoms were in order to be taken seriously. Some patients used previous psychiatric hospitalisations as proof or support for the seriousness of their condition. Others were hesitant to tell their doctor about their suicidal thoughts because they were afraid of hospitalisation or not being taken seriously. Patients shared that they had to go through many obstacles to get the treatment they wanted.

According to participants, health care providers thought it was acceptable or normal for women to experience some degree of menstrual pain and suffering.

In addition, female participants reported concerns about doctors' practice of to prescribe first-line medications because doctors prescribed them without follow-up. If there was follow-up, it was most often after 9 weeks, which participants said was sufficient time for them to feel suicidal intentions after the negative side effects of the medication.

Thus, you may face either queues at hospitals or misunderstanding of doctors. But what to do? You need your health right now. If you don't take care of your health, it will increase your risk of developing cardiovascular disease, diabetes, high blood pressure and other chronic conditions. Uncontrolled stress can lead to depression, anxiety and other mental disorders. Without the right remedy, the immune system will deteriorate, making the body more susceptible to infections and diseases. Overall life expectancy is also shortened due to regular stress!

Women who are chronically stressed also have significantly lower levels of the hormone Klotho. Klotho is an important hormone that performs many different functions in the body. Not only does it improve cognitive abilities, but it also regulates aging. This discovery suggests a powerful link between stress and longevity. A study conducted at the University of California, San Francisco found many interesting results regarding the relationship between stress, depression and klotho.

A senior author named Dena Dubal, MD, PHD, and her team worked together to get some new evidence regarding klotho. She found that, essentially, the more klotho, the better - and some people are born with a genetic variant that results in more klotho in the blood. These people tended to live longer, have lower rates of age-related diseases, a larger prefrontal cortex area and better cognitive function. Chronic stress in general has been shown to lead to health problems during aging. These problems include Alzheimer's disease in addition to cardiovascular disease.
